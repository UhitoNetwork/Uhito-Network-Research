# Investigation Questions

## A. For Hackers
1. What was your first unauthorized access, and how did it make you feel?
2. Who taught you, or where did you learn your skills?
3. When did you realize hacking could have real-world consequences for real people?
4. What would make you stop hacking illegally?
5. If you could use your skills to protect instead of attack, would you? What would make that appealing?
6. Have you ever tried to report a vulnerability legally? What happened?
7. What do you wish authorities understood about hackers?
8. Where do you see yourself in 5 years?

## B. For Authorities
1. Do you see hackers primarily as criminals or potential allies?
2. How many legal hacking labs or safe practice environments exist in your jurisdiction?
3. Do you actively recruit from hacker communities, or only monitor them for threats?
4. What is your strategy for redirecting young hacking talent toward positive contributions?
5. Is prevention and education part of your cybercrime policy, or is it purely reactive?
6. How do you build trust with technical communities that distrust authority?
7. What barriers exist to hiring self-taught or unconventional cybersecurity talent?
8. Have you ever worked successfully with a reformed hacker? What made it work?

## C. For Society
1. Are most parents aware of what their children are capable of doing online?
2. Do schools teach digital ethics and responsibility alongside technical skills?
3. Does media coverage glorify hackers as rebels or demonize them as criminals?
4. Are there sufficient safe spaces for curious minds to explore cybersecurity legally?
5. Who bears responsibility when a young person becomes a hacker: individual, parents, schools, or society?
6. How can communities better identify and support at-risk technical talent?
7. What role should corporations play in cybersecurity education and talent development?
8. Is cybersecurity seen as a national priority requiring citizen participation?
